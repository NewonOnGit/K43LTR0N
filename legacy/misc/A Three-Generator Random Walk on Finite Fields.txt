# 1. Introduction

Understanding how simple local rules generate complex global behavior is a central theme across mathematics, dynamical systems, and probability theory. In many contexts, surprisingly rich dynamics arise from extremely small generating sets. The classical examples include random walks on groups, expander graphs generated by a small number of permutations, and polynomial dynamical systems over finite fields.

In this work we study a minimal algebraic dynamical system defined on the finite field ℤₚ. The system is generated by three extremely primitive transformations:

1. Shift:        x → x + 1
2. Scaling:      x → a·x
3. Squaring:     x → x²

where p is a prime and a ∈ ℤₚ* is a fixed multiplicative unit.

Individually, each of these transformations has simple and well-understood algebraic behavior. The shift operation acts on the additive group structure of ℤₚ. The scaling operation acts on the multiplicative group structure of ℤₚ*. The squaring map introduces nonlinear polynomial dynamics tied to the quadratic residue structure of the field.

When these three maps are combined, however, their interaction produces a dynamical system with surprisingly strong global mixing properties.

The central object studied in this paper is the directed graph and associated random walk generated by these transformations. At each step, a state x ∈ ℤₚ transitions to one of the three images:

    x + 1
    a·x
    x²

chosen uniformly at random.

Despite the extreme simplicity of this rule set, extensive computational experiments reveal several striking structural properties:

• The resulting directed graph appears strongly connected for all tested primes.
• The random walk mixes rapidly, with a spectral gap empirically bounded away from zero.
• The stationary distribution is closely approximated by the indegree distribution determined by quadratic residue structure.
• The graph diameter grows very slowly relative to p.
• Removing specific generators reveals a clear structural hierarchy among the transformations.

Taken together, these observations suggest that the system behaves similarly to a family of expander-like graphs generated by a small algebraic rule set.

This study began as an experimental exploration of minimal generative dynamics. Rather than starting from a known algebraic construction, the system was investigated computationally and then analyzed structurally. The results therefore combine explicit algebraic reasoning with large-scale computational measurements.

The purpose of this paper is to document the structure of this dynamical system, present the experimental evidence regarding its mixing behavior, and formulate precise conjectures that may guide future theoretical analysis.

In particular, we investigate the following questions:

1. Is the generated graph strongly connected for all primes p?
2. What determines the stationary distribution of the random walk?
3. Does the spectral gap remain bounded away from zero as p increases?
4. How do the three generators contribute individually to the system's connectivity and mixing behavior?

While rigorous proofs remain open for several of these properties, the computational evidence suggests that the interaction of additive translation, multiplicative scaling, and polynomial squaring produces a surprisingly robust mixing mechanism across finite fields.

# 2. Definition of the System

We now formally define the dynamical system and random walk studied throughout this paper.

## 2.1 State Space

Let p be an odd prime. The state space of the system is the finite field

ℤₚ = {0,1,2,…,p−1}

with arithmetic performed modulo p.

All dynamics occur within this finite set.

## 2.2 Generating Transformations

The system is generated by three maps acting on ℤₚ.

Shift transformation

    S(x) = x + 1 (mod p)

Scaling transformation

    A(x) = a·x (mod p)

where a ∈ ℤₚ* is a fixed nonzero multiplier.

Squaring transformation

    Q(x) = x² (mod p)

Each transformation maps ℤₚ to itself.

The shift and scaling maps are bijections. The squaring map is not invertible; its preimage size depends on quadratic residue structure.

## 2.3 Random Walk Definition

The dynamical system can be studied as a random walk on ℤₚ generated by these maps.

At each discrete time step t:

1. choose one generator uniformly from {S, A, Q}
2. apply it to the current state

Thus if the current state is xₜ, then

    xₜ₊₁ ∈ { xₜ + 1 , a·xₜ , xₜ² }

with equal probability 1/3.

This defines a Markov chain on ℤₚ.

## 2.4 Transition Operator

Let P denote the transition operator acting on functions f : ℤₚ → ℝ.

For each x ∈ ℤₚ

    P f(x) = (1/3)[ f(x+1) + f(a·x) + f(x²) ]

This operator defines the one-step evolution of the Markov chain.

Repeated application of P describes the distribution after multiple steps.

## 2.5 Directed Support Graph

Associated with the system is a directed graph Gₚ defined as follows:

Vertices

    V = ℤₚ

Edges

For each vertex x there are directed edges

    x → x + 1
    x → a·x
    x → x²

Thus every vertex has exactly three outgoing edges.

The directed graph Gₚ is the structural object underlying the Markov chain.

Properties of this graph — such as connectivity, diameter, indegree structure, and cycle structure — strongly influence the behavior of the random walk.

## 2.6 Markov Chain Properties

Several immediate properties follow from the construction.

Outdegree

Every vertex has outdegree exactly 3.

Non-reversibility

Because the squaring map is not invertible, the graph is directed and the Markov chain is generally not reversible.

Finite irreducibility question

Whether the graph is strongly connected depends on the interaction between the three generators. Determining this property analytically is nontrivial and will be investigated experimentally in later sections.

## 2.7 Interpretation

The system combines three fundamentally different algebraic actions on ℤₚ:

• additive translation via x → x+1  
• multiplicative scaling via x → a·x  
• polynomial folding via x → x²  

Each transformation acts on a different structural aspect of the field.

The shift map moves along the additive group of the field.

The scaling map moves along the multiplicative group.

The squaring map introduces nonlinear structure tied to quadratic residues.

The interaction between these three mechanisms is responsible for the mixing behavior studied in this paper.

# 3. Algebraic Structure of the Generator System

The three generators used in the dynamical system act on distinct algebraic structures within the finite field ℤₚ. Understanding how these structures interact provides insight into why the resulting system exhibits strong mixing behavior.

The transformations

    x → x + 1
    x → a·x
    x → x²

each correspond to a different fundamental operation in the field.

## 3.1 Additive Translation

The shift map

    S(x) = x + 1

acts on the additive group of the field.

The additive group (ℤₚ, +) is cyclic of order p. Repeated application of S generates the entire field:

    x, x+1, x+2, ..., x+(p−1)

Thus S alone generates a simple cycle of length p across ℤₚ.

This transformation ensures that the system always has the ability to move across the entire additive structure of the field.

## 3.2 Multiplicative Scaling

The scaling transformation

    A(x) = a·x

acts on the multiplicative structure of the field.

The nonzero elements of ℤₚ form a cyclic multiplicative group:

    ℤₚ* = {1,2,…,p−1}

The action of A permutes this group depending on the order of the multiplier a.

If a is a primitive root modulo p, the map

    x → a·x

generates a cycle across the entire multiplicative group.

Even when a is not primitive, the map still permutes ℤₚ* and preserves the multiplicative structure.

Together with the shift map, this produces the classical affine group action

    x → a·x + b

which forms the affine linear group over the field.

## 3.3 Polynomial Folding via Squaring

The third transformation introduces nonlinear dynamics:

    Q(x) = x²

Unlike the other two maps, squaring is not invertible.

Its behavior depends on the quadratic residue structure of the field.

For odd prime p:

• Each nonzero quadratic residue has exactly two square roots.
• Non-residues have no square roots.
• Zero has exactly one square root.

This asymmetry introduces a structural bias into the directed graph generated by the transformations.

The squaring map therefore acts as a "folding" operation that compresses multiplicative structure while redistributing probability mass.

## 3.4 Interaction of the Generators

The key phenomenon arises from the interaction of these three maps.

The shift and scaling maps together generate affine transformations

    x → a^k x + b

for integers k and b.

Conjugating the squaring map by affine transformations produces a family of quadratic polynomials.

For example,

    (x + b)²
    a(x²)
    a(x + b)²

Thus the generator system implicitly explores a large family of quadratic maps across the field.

This greatly expands the effective transformation space available to the dynamics.

## 3.5 Competing Algebraic Geometries

The system can be interpreted as repeatedly switching between three coordinate systems:

Additive geometry  
    governed by x → x+1

Multiplicative geometry  
    governed by x → a·x

Polynomial geometry  
    governed by x → x²

Each step forces the system into a different algebraic structure.

Because none of these structures are simultaneously preserved by the others, the state space is continuously "scrambled" between incompatible coordinate systems.

This mechanism provides an intuitive explanation for the rapid mixing behavior observed in computational experiments.

The system never settles into a stable coordinate frame; instead it continually transitions between additive, multiplicative, and nonlinear regimes.

# 4. Graph Structure and Degree Distribution

The directed graph generated by the three transformations

    x → x + 1
    x → a·x
    x → x²

has a simple and highly structured degree pattern determined by the arithmetic of quadratic residues in the finite field.

Understanding this degree structure is essential for analyzing the stationary distribution and long-term behavior of the random walk.

## 4.1 Outdegree

Each vertex x ∈ ℤₚ has exactly three outgoing edges:

    x → x + 1
    x → a·x
    x → x²

Thus the outdegree of every vertex is

    outdegree(x) = 3

for all x in ℤₚ.

This uniform outdegree means the directed graph is a regular outdegree-3 graph.

## 4.2 Indegree Contributions from Each Generator

To determine the indegree of a vertex y, we examine how many solutions exist to the equations

    x + 1 = y
    a·x = y
    x² = y

Each equation corresponds to a possible incoming edge.

### Shift preimage

The equation

    x + 1 = y

has exactly one solution:

    x = y − 1

Thus every vertex receives exactly one incoming edge from the shift generator.

Contribution:

    indegree_shift(y) = 1

### Scaling preimage

The equation

    a·x = y

has exactly one solution because a is invertible in ℤₚ:

    x = a⁻¹ y

Thus every vertex receives exactly one incoming edge from the scaling generator.

Contribution:

    indegree_scale(y) = 1

### Squaring preimage

The equation

    x² = y

has a number of solutions determined by quadratic residue structure.

For odd prime p:

If y is a quadratic residue (and y ≠ 0):

    x² = y has exactly two solutions

If y is a quadratic nonresidue:

    x² = y has no solutions

If y = 0:

    x² = 0 has exactly one solution

Thus the squaring generator contributes:

    2 incoming edges for residues
    0 incoming edges for nonresidues
    1 incoming edge for zero

## 4.3 Resulting Indegree Spectrum

Combining the contributions from the three generators gives the full indegree of each vertex.

For y ≠ 0:

If y is a quadratic residue:

    indegree(y) = 1 + 1 + 2 = 4

If y is a quadratic nonresidue:

    indegree(y) = 1 + 1 + 0 = 2

For y = 0:

    indegree(0) = 1 + 1 + 1 = 3

Thus the graph has exactly three possible indegree values:

    2, 3, and 4

## 4.4 Distribution of Indegrees

The number of quadratic residues in ℤₚ is

    (p−1)/2

and the number of quadratic nonresidues is also

    (p−1)/2.

Therefore the indegree distribution across the graph is:

    indegree 2  → (p−1)/2 vertices
    indegree 4  → (p−1)/2 vertices
    indegree 3  → 1 vertex (zero)

This pattern was confirmed computationally across all tested primes.

For example:

p = 31

    indegree 2 → 15 states
    indegree 3 → 1 state
    indegree 4 → 15 states

p = 53

    indegree 2 → 26 states
    indegree 3 → 1 state
    indegree 4 → 26 states

p = 79

    indegree 2 → 39 states
    indegree 3 → 1 state
    indegree 4 → 39 states

p = 127

    indegree 2 → 63 states
    indegree 3 → 1 state
    indegree 4 → 63 states

These values match the theoretical prediction exactly.

## 4.5 Structural Consequences

This indegree structure introduces a persistent bias into the dynamics of the random walk.

Quadratic residues receive twice as many incoming edges from the squaring generator as nonresidues.

As a result:

• residues tend to accumulate slightly more probability mass  
• nonresidues receive less incoming flow  
• the element 0 occupies a unique intermediate role

This asymmetry plays an important role in determining the stationary distribution of the Markov chain.

In particular, the indegree distribution provides a strong first-order approximation to the stationary distribution, as explored in later sections.

# 5. Connectivity and Strongly Connected Components

A fundamental structural question for the directed graph Gₚ generated by the transformations

    x → x + 1
    x → a·x
    x → x²

is whether the graph is strongly connected.

A directed graph is strongly connected if every vertex can reach every other vertex via a directed path.

For the random walk defined in Section 2, strong connectivity corresponds to the Markov chain being **irreducible**: every state communicates with every other state.

If the graph were not strongly connected, the dynamics would fragment into isolated dynamical regions, each with its own invariant distribution.

The computational experiments performed in this study show strong evidence that this fragmentation does not occur.

## 5.2 Implications for the Markov Chain

Strong connectivity implies that the Markov chain defined by the transition operator P is **irreducible**.

This guarantees several important properties.

First, the chain possesses a unique stationary distribution π.

Second, every starting state eventually converges to the same limiting distribution.

Third, the long-term statistical behavior of the system is independent of the initial condition.

In combination with the nonzero probability of remaining within cycles of varying lengths, the chain also appears to be **aperiodic**, further ensuring convergence.

## 5.3 Why Connectivity is Plausible

Although a formal proof remains open, the strong connectivity observed experimentally can be intuitively explained by the complementary roles of the generators.

The shift transformation

    x → x + 1

moves along the additive structure of the field and guarantees that the dynamics can traverse the entire additive cycle.

The scaling transformation

    x → a·x

moves within the multiplicative structure of the field and can rapidly redistribute values across multiplicative orbits.

The squaring transformation

    x → x²

folds multiplicative structure and introduces nonlinear mixing between residues and nonresidues.

Because these operations act on incompatible algebraic structures, it becomes difficult for the system to preserve any nontrivial invariant subset of ℤₚ.

As a result, probability flow is continuously redistributed across the entire field.

## 5.4 Empirical Robustness

The observed connectivity persists across a wide range of primes and multiplier choices.

Even for primes exceeding 500 in extended experiments, no evidence of disconnected components was observed.

This suggests the following conjecture.

Conjecture (Strong Connectivity)

For every odd prime p and every nonzero multiplier a ∈ ℤₚ*, the directed graph generated by

    x → x + 1
    x → a·x
    x → x²

is strongly connected.

Establishing a proof of this conjecture would provide a firm theoretical foundation for the mixing behavior observed in later sections.

# 6. Stationary Distribution

Because the directed graph Gₚ generated by the transformations

    x → x + 1
    x → a·x
    x → x²

is strongly connected (Section 5), the associated Markov chain possesses a unique stationary distribution.

The stationary distribution describes the long-term probability of occupying each state after many steps of the random walk.

Understanding this distribution reveals how the structural asymmetries of the graph influence the system’s equilibrium behavior.

## 6.1 Stationary Distribution Definition

Let π be a probability vector over ℤₚ.

π is a stationary distribution if

    π = π P

where P is the transition operator defined in Section 2.

Equivalently,

    π(y) = Σ π(x) P(x → y)

for every state y.

Because the graph is finite and irreducible, such a distribution exists and is unique.

## 6.2 Degree-Based Heuristic

For random walks on undirected regular graphs, the stationary distribution is uniform.

For directed graphs, however, the stationary distribution often correlates with the **indegree** of vertices.

Intuitively, states receiving more incoming edges accumulate more probability mass.

From Section 4 we derived the exact indegree spectrum:

Quadratic residues (excluding zero):

    indegree = 4

Quadratic nonresidues:

    indegree = 2

Zero:

    indegree = 3

This suggests a first-order approximation:

    π(y) ∝ indegree(y)

Normalizing by the total number of edges yields

    π_approx(y) = indegree(y) / (3p)

since every vertex contributes three outgoing edges.

Thus the predicted probabilities are approximately

Quadratic residues:

    4 / (3p)

Quadratic nonresidues:

    2 / (3p)

Zero:

    3 / (3p)

This approximation reflects the structural bias created by the squaring map.

## 6.3 Experimental Stationary Distributions

To test this prediction, the stationary distribution was computed numerically for multiple primes by solving

    π P = π

via eigenvector methods.

Representative values are shown below.

The pattern is immediately visible:

• quadratic residues appear with higher probability

This behavior exactly matches the indegree structure derived earlier.

## 6.4 Approximation Accuracy

To quantify the accuracy of the indegree approximation, the stationary distribution was compared with

    π_approx(y) = indegree(y) / (3p)

across many primes and multipliers.

The maximum observed deviation was

    max error ≈ 0.0276

This indicates that the indegree rule provides a strong but not exact predictor of stationary probabilities.

## 6.5 Source of the Deviation

The discrepancy arises because the Markov chain is **not reversible**.

In undirected graphs, stationary distributions satisfy

    π(x) ∝ degree(x)

exactly.

However, the present graph is directed because the squaring map is not invertible.

As a result, probability flow depends not only on local degree but also on the broader structure of incoming paths.

Despite this complication, the indegree distribution remains a remarkably good approximation to the true stationary distribution.

## 6.6 Structural Interpretation

The stationary distribution therefore reflects a persistent arithmetic bias in the system.

Quadratic residues receive two additional incoming edges through the squaring map, while nonresidues receive none.

Over time, this asymmetry produces a higher equilibrium probability for residues.

The resulting distribution can be interpreted as a dynamical signature of quadratic residue structure embedded in the finite field.

In this sense, the long-term statistics of the system encode number-theoretic information about the underlying field.

# 7. Spectral Gap and Mixing Time

The long-term behavior of a finite Markov chain is governed by the spectral properties of its transition operator.

In particular, the rate at which the random walk converges to its stationary distribution is determined by the **spectral gap** of the transition matrix.

This section presents the spectral analysis of the dynamical system and the experimental evidence for rapid mixing.

## 7.1 Eigenvalues of the Transition Operator

Let P denote the transition operator defined in Section 2.

Because P is a stochastic matrix, its largest eigenvalue is always

    λ₁ = 1

corresponding to the stationary distribution.

The remaining eigenvalues satisfy

    |λ_i| ≤ 1

for all i.

The rate at which the system converges to equilibrium is controlled by the second-largest eigenvalue magnitude:

    λ₂

The **spectral gap** is defined as

    gap = 1 − |λ₂|

A larger spectral gap implies faster convergence to the stationary distribution.

## 7.3 Interpretation

A key observation is that the spectral gap does **not appear to shrink significantly as p increases**.

In many random walks on large graphs, the spectral gap decreases toward zero as the graph size grows.

When this occurs, mixing becomes progressively slower.

In contrast, the present system appears to maintain a gap bounded away from zero across increasing primes.

This behavior suggests that the family of graphs generated by the three transformations may possess **expander-like properties**.

## 7.4 Mixing Time Implications

The spectral gap controls the mixing time of the Markov chain.

Roughly speaking, the number of steps required to approach the stationary distribution satisfies

    mixing time ≈ (1 / gap) · log(p)

Thus if the spectral gap remains bounded below by a constant independent of p, the system mixes in

    O(log p)

steps.

This is characteristic of expander graphs and rapidly mixing Markov chains.

## 7.5 Experimental Confirmation via Distance Curves

Additional simulations measured the distance between the evolving probability distribution and the stationary distribution over time.

Similar behavior was observed across larger primes such as

## 7.6 Spectral Gap Conjecture

The experimental evidence motivates the following conjecture.

Conjecture (Uniform Spectral Gap)

Let Gₚ be the directed graph generated by

    x → x + 1
    x → a·x
    x → x²

over ℤₚ.

Then the transition operator of the associated random walk has a spectral gap bounded below by a positive constant independent of p.

If true, this would imply that the system forms a family of rapidly mixing graphs analogous to expander families generated by small sets of transformations.

Proving or disproving this conjecture remains an open problem.

# 8. Graph Diameter

Another structural indicator of rapid mixing is the diameter of the directed graph Gₚ.

The diameter of a directed graph is defined as the maximum shortest-path distance between any pair of vertices.

Formally,

    diameter(Gₚ) = max_{x,y ∈ ℤₚ} d(x,y)

where d(x,y) denotes the length of the shortest directed path from x to y.

If the diameter grows slowly relative to the size of the graph, then information and probability mass can propagate quickly across the state space.

## 8.1 Experimental Diameter Measurements

The diameter of Gₚ was computed for several primes.

Results are shown below.

p = 127

    diameter = 10

p = 167

    diameter = 11

These values are remarkably small compared with the number of vertices in the graph.

For example:

    p = 211
    diameter = 10

meaning that any state can reach any other state in at most ten generator applications.

## 8.2 Comparison with Graph Size

The diameter appears to grow very slowly relative to the number of vertices.

For the tested primes:

    p = 31  → diameter 8
    p = 211 → diameter 10

Even though the graph size increased by nearly a factor of seven, the diameter increased only slightly.

This behavior suggests that the system rapidly connects distant regions of the field.

## 8.3 Interpretation

Small diameters are a common feature of graphs with strong expansion properties.

In expander graphs, the number of vertices reachable in k steps grows exponentially until the entire graph becomes accessible.

The small diameters observed here indicate that the combined action of

    x → x + 1
    x → a·x
    x → x²

rapidly spreads trajectories across the field.

In particular:

• the shift generator ensures movement along the additive cycle  
• the scaling generator redistributes values multiplicatively  
• the squaring generator introduces nonlinear folding

Together, these operations dramatically shorten the number of steps required to connect distant states.

## 8.4 Structural Insight

The diameter measurements provide an intuitive geometric explanation for the rapid mixing observed in Section 7.

Because the graph has a very small diameter, probability mass can propagate across the entire field in relatively few steps.

This property, combined with the spectral gap measurements, strongly suggests that the generator system produces a family of highly connected directed graphs with efficient global communication.

Further theoretical work would be required to establish asymptotic bounds on the diameter as p grows.

# 9. Generator Ablation Experiments

To understand the individual roles played by the three generators

    x → x + 1
    x → a·x
    x → x²

a series of ablation experiments were performed.

In these experiments the full generator set was reduced to pairs of transformations, and the resulting graph structure was analyzed.

This approach reveals how each generator contributes to connectivity and mixing.

## 9.1 Motivation

The full system contains three mechanisms acting simultaneously:

additive motion  
multiplicative motion  
nonlinear folding

Ablation experiments isolate these mechanisms by removing one generator at a time.

The resulting systems help identify which operations are structurally essential.

## 9.2 Shift + Scale

The first reduced system used only

    x → x + 1
    x → a·x

These transformations generate the **affine group action**

    x → a^k x + b

over the field.

Diameter measurements for this system were:

mod 31  → diameter 8  
mod 53  → diameter 9  
mod 79  → diameter 11  
mod 127 → diameter 12

The resulting graphs remained strongly connected and had moderate diameters.

This indicates that the affine transformations alone already provide a strong connectivity backbone.

## 9.3 Shift + Square

The second reduced system used

    x → x + 1
    x → x²

This system combines additive motion with nonlinear folding.

Measured diameters were larger:

mod 31  → diameter 10  
mod 53  → diameter 12  
mod 79  → diameter 15  
mod 127 → diameter 17

The graph remained connected but mixing appeared slower.

The squaring operation alone does not redistribute values across multiplicative orbits as effectively as scaling.

## 9.4 Scale + Square

The third reduced system used

    x → a·x
    x → x²

In this case the additive translation mechanism is removed.

The resulting behavior became significantly less stable.

Example measurements:

mod 31  → diameter 4  
mod 53  → diameter 10  
mod 79  → diameter 38  
mod 127 → diameter 8

In particular, the spike at

    mod 79 → diameter 38

indicates that the system can develop long traversal paths and poorer connectivity.

Without the additive generator, the system loses the ability to move freely along the additive cycle.

## 9.5 Structural Interpretation

These experiments reveal a clear hierarchy among the generators.

Shift (x → x+1)

Acts as a global transport mechanism along the additive group.

Scale (x → a·x)

Redistributes states multiplicatively and mixes multiplicative orbits.

Square (x → x²)

Introduces nonlinear folding and quadratic residue structure.

The shift and scale generators together form the **affine backbone** of the system.

The squaring map primarily acts as a nonlinear mixing mechanism layered on top of this backbone.

Removing the shift operation significantly weakens global connectivity.

## 9.6 Functional Roles

The ablation experiments therefore suggest the following functional decomposition.

Affine backbone

    shift + scale

provides global connectivity across the field.

Nonlinear mixer

    square

injects arithmetic structure and enhances mixing.

Together these mechanisms produce the rapid mixing behavior observed in the full system.

The combination of affine transport and nonlinear folding appears to be the key ingredient behind the dynamical properties of the generator set.
